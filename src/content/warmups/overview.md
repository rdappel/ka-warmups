# Adult Karate Pre-Class Warm-Up Guide

## Purpose

These warm-ups are designed to prepare students for class, not exhaust them. Each routine should take approximately **10-12 minutes** and should leave students warmer, looser, more focused, and ready to train.

The routines are built for adult karate classes with mixed ages, ranks, and abilities. A white belt, a teenager, a senior student, and a multi-degree black belt should all be able to participate together. The instructor may provide lower-impact or higher-intensity versions when appropriate, but the class should remain unified.

## Warm-Up Philosophy

A good pre-class warm-up should:

1. Raise body temperature gradually.
2. Move major joints through comfortable ranges of motion.
3. Activate muscles used in karate.
4. Prepare common injury-prone areas: shoulders, wrists, hips, knees, ankles, and low back.
5. Transition students mentally and physically into karate movement.

The warm-up is not conditioning class. Avoid turning the first 10 minutes into a workout that leaves students tired before instruction begins.

## Standard Routine Structure

Each routine uses the same five-part structure:

### 1. Raise Body Temperature
Light movement to increase heart rate and blood flow.

### 2. Joint Mobility
Controlled joint movement for the neck, shoulders, wrists, hips, knees, and ankles.

### 3. Dynamic Mobility & Activation
Active movement that prepares the muscles and joints for stances, kicking, blocking, striking, and movement.

### 4. Karate Integration
Simple karate-based movement patterns such as punches, blocks, stance transitions, footwork, and controlled kicks.

### 5. Finish / Reset
A short transition into class. This may include breathing, a light stretch, basic techniques, or light kicking.

## Design Rules

- No equipment required.
- No partner required.
- Every routine should work for mixed-rank adult classes.
- Space-consuming movements should include a small-space option.
- Karate techniques should remain fundamental.
- Fitness intensity may scale up or down.
- Students should move within a comfortable range of motion.
- Quality matters more than speed.
- The instructor should demonstrate both the standard version and a safer modification when needed.

## Modification Philosophy

The goal is not for everyone to perform the hardest version of an exercise. The goal is for everyone to keep moving safely.

Examples:

- Jumping jacks may become step jacks.
- Burpees may become squat thrusts or squat-to-stand.
- Kicks may stay low.
- Lunges may become shorter or shallower.
- Hops may become steps.

Use language like:

> Choose the version that lets you move well today.

Avoid singling students out with language like:

> If you can't do this, do the easy version.
